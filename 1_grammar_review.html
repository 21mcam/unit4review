Past Unreal Conditional

TEST QUESTIONS 30, 32, 35

The past unreal conditional (also called the third conditional) describes situations that did not happen in the past. It asks: "What would have happened if things had been different?"

The Formula:
If + had + past participle → would have + past participle

Example: If I had studied, I would have passed the test.

The most common mistake is using "would be" or "would + base verb" in the result clause instead of "would have + past participle."

If I had been there, I would be so upset.
If I had been there, I would have been so upset.
If she had not fought, the country would be turned out differently.
If she had not fought, the country would have turned out differently.

This also applies to hypothetical questions about the past:

What do you think you did during those years?
What do you think you would have done during those years?

Parallel Structure in Verb Lists

TEST QUESTION 31

When you list actions in a sentence using and or or, every verb in the list must be in the same tense. This is called parallel structure.

The Rule: All verbs joined by "and" or "or" in a series must match in form.

Think of it like a team: If one verb is past tense, they all need to be past tense.
They held protests, marched, and had been arrested.
They held protests, marched, and were arrested.

Notice how "held" and "marched" are simple past tense. The third verb needs to match — "were arrested" (simple past, passive) — not "had been arrested" (past perfect).

She sang, danced, and has performed for the crowd.
She sang, danced, and performed for the crowd.

Infinitives of Purpose

TEST QUESTIONS 33, 34

An infinitive of purpose tells why something was done. It uses the word "to" followed by the base form of a verb.

Pattern: [action] + to + [base verb]

Means: "in order to"

Example: Laws were enacted to ensure equal rights. (= in order to ensure)

A common error is using "of ensuring" or "for ensure" instead of the correct infinitive "to ensure."

Restrictions were enacted of ensuring that few could vote.
Restrictions were enacted to ensure that few could vote.

You also need to know when an infinitive is already correct. On the test, "the right to vote" is correct as-is — don't change a correct infinitive to something like "of vote" or "of voting."

the right of voting
the right to vote ← already correct!

"Would Have" vs. "Would Of"

TEST QUESTION 32

This is one of the most common grammar mistakes in English, and it shows up on the test. People write "would of" because that's what "would've" sounds like when spoken aloud. But "would of" is always wrong.

The Rule: It's always would have, never would of.

This applies to could have, should have, and might have too.
The country would of turned out differently.
The country would have turned out differently.
He could of escaped sooner.
He could have escaped sooner.
They should of fought harder.
They should have fought harder.

Why does this happen? When we say "would've" out loud, the contraction "-'ve" sounds just like "of." But "of" is a preposition — it has nothing to do with verbs. The correct word is always "have."
